Quick Start Guide

Follow these essential steps to get your Australian business up and running quickly:

5 Key Steps to Start Your Business

Choose Your Business Structure

Select the most appropriate business structure based on your needs:

  • Sole Trader: Simplest structure with complete control but unlimited liability
  • Partnership: Shared ownership between 2-20 people with joint liability
  • Company: Separate legal entity with limited liability but more complex to set up
  • Trust: Complex structure where a trustee holds assets for beneficiaries

Learn more about business structures

Apply for an Australian Business Number (ABN)

An ABN is essential for all businesses in Australia:

  • Free to apply through the Australian Business Register
  • Required before registering a business name or GST
  • 11-digit unique identifier for your business
  • Allows you to claim GST credits and avoid PAYG withholding

Prepare your identification documents and business details before applying.

Register Your Business Name

If you're trading under a name other than your personal name:

  • Register through ASIC or the Business Registration Service
  • Check name availability before registering
  • Registration fees apply ($37 for 1 year or $88 for 3 years)
  • Consider trademark protection for your business name

Note: Registering a business name doesn't give you exclusive rights to that name.

Register for Tax Obligations

Depending on your business structure and turnover:

  • GST: Mandatory if turnover exceeds $75,000 ($150,000 for non-profits)
  • PAYG Withholding: Required if you have employees
  • Tax File Number (TFN): Essential for all business types
  • Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT): If you provide benefits to employees

Register through the Australian Taxation Office or the Business Registration Service.

Obtain Necessary Licenses and Permits

Research and apply for industry-specific licenses:

  • Use the Australian Business License and Information Service (ABLIS) to identify required licenses
  • Check local council permits for your business premises
  • Obtain industry-specific certifications and registrations
  • Ensure compliance with health, safety, and environmental regulations

Licenses and permits vary by industry and location, so research thoroughly.

Business Types in Australia

Australia offers several business structures, each with different legal and tax implications. Choose the structure that best suits your business goals and circumstances:

Sole Trader

A sole trader is the simplest business structure in Australia, where you operate the business as an individual and are legally responsible for all aspects of the business.

Advantages

  • Simple and inexpensive to establish
  • Complete control over business decisions
  • Minimal regulatory obligations
  • Easy to change business structure later
  • All profits belong to you
  • Simple tax arrangements

Disadvantages

  • Unlimited personal liability for business debts
  • Business income taxed at personal income rates
  • Limited access to capital for growth
  • Business ceases to exist if you die
  • Difficult to sell the business as an entity

Registration Requirements

  • Apply for an Australian Business Number (ABN)
  • Register your business name (if not using your personal name)
  • Register for GST if turnover exceeds $75,000
  • Obtain necessary licenses and permits

Partnership

A partnership is a business structure where 2-20 people share ownership and responsibility for the business.

Advantages

  • Relatively simple and inexpensive to set up
  • Shared workload and complementary skills
  • Minimal reporting requirements
  • Shared startup costs and resources
  • Tax advantages through income splitting
  • Broader management base

Disadvantages

  • Joint liability for business debts
  • Each partner is liable for the actions of other partners
  • Potential for disputes between partners
  • Partnership dissolves if a partner leaves
  • Profits must be shared
  • Decision-making can be complex

Registration Requirements

  • Create a partnership agreement (recommended but not legally required)
  • Apply for a Tax File Number (TFN) for the partnership
  • Apply for an Australian Business Number (ABN)
  • Register your business name
  • Register for GST if turnover exceeds $75,000

Company

A company is a separate legal entity from its owners, with its own rights and responsibilities. The most common type in Australia is a Proprietary Limited (Pty Ltd) company.

Advantages

  • Limited liability for shareholders
  • Separate legal entity that can own assets
  • Flat company tax rate (25-30%)
  • Easier to raise capital and transfer ownership
  • Perpetual existence independent of shareholders
  • Enhanced business credibility

Disadvantages

  • More expensive and complex to establish
  • Significant regulatory and reporting obligations
  • Higher setup and compliance costs
  • Directors have legal responsibilities
  • Less privacy (company details are public)
  • Complex tax arrangements

Registration Requirements

  • Register with the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC)
  • Appoint directors and shareholders
  • Create a company constitution or adopt replaceable rules
  • Apply for an Australian Company Number (ACN)
  • Apply for an Australian Business Number (ABN)
  • Register for GST if turnover exceeds $75,000

Trust

A trust is a structure where a trustee (an individual or company) carries out the business on behalf of the trust's beneficiaries.

Advantages

  • Asset protection benefits
  • Potential tax advantages through income distribution
  • Flexibility in distributing income
  • Separation of control and beneficial ownership
  • Capital gains tax concessions
  • Estate planning benefits

Disadvantages

  • Complex and expensive to establish
  • Requires a formal trust deed
  • Ongoing administrative complexity
  • Losses cannot be distributed to beneficiaries
  • Higher setup and maintenance costs
  • Limited lifespan (usually 80 years)

Registration Requirements

  • Create a formal trust deed
  • Appoint a trustee
  • Apply for a Tax File Number (TFN) for the trust
  • Apply for an Australian Business Number (ABN)
  • Register your business name
  • Register for GST if turnover exceeds $75,000

Sole Trader

  • Simplest and least expensive structure
  • Complete control over business decisions
  • Personal liability for business debts
  • Business income taxed as personal income
  • Suitable for small, low-risk businesses

Partnership

  • Shared ownership between 2-20 people
  • Relatively simple and inexpensive to set up
  • Partners share profits, losses, and responsibilities
  • Joint liability for business debts
  • Partnership agreement recommended

Company

  • Separate legal entity from its owners
  • Limited liability for shareholders
  • More complex and expensive to establish
  • Subject to additional regulations and reporting
  • Flat company tax rate (25-30%)

Trust

  • Complex structure with a trustee
  • Trustee holds assets for beneficiaries
  • Potential tax advantages
  • Higher setup and maintenance costs
  • Requires formal trust deed

Registration Process

Registering your business in Australia involves several steps depending on your chosen business structure. Follow this guide to ensure you complete all necessary registrations:

Business registration process overview

1

Choose Your Business Structure

Decide whether you'll operate as a sole trader, partnership, company, or trust based on your business needs, liability considerations, and tax implications.

2

Apply for an Australian Business Number (ABN)

An ABN is essential for all businesses in Australia. Apply online through the Australian Business Register. The application is free and can be completed in one session.

ABN Australian Business Number: An 11-digit identifier issued by the Australian Business Register that identifies your business to the government and community.

3

Register Your Business Name

If you're trading under a name other than your personal name, you must register it with the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC). Registration fees apply and renewal is required every 1 or 3 years.

4

Register for Tax Obligations

Depending on your business structure and turnover, you may need to register for:

  • Goods and Services Tax (GST) A broad-based tax of 10% on most goods, services, and other items sold or consumed in Australia. Registration is mandatory if your turnover exceeds $75,000. - mandatory if turnover exceeds $75,000
  • Pay As You Go (PAYG) withholding A system where employers withhold tax from payments made to employees and certain contractors, then remit these amounts to the ATO. - if you have employees
  • Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT) A tax paid by employers on certain benefits they provide to employees or their associates in addition to salary or wages. - if you provide benefits to employees
5

Obtain Necessary Licenses and Permits

Research and apply for any industry-specific licenses, permits, or registrations required for your business operations. Requirements vary by industry and location.

Use the Australian Business License and Information Service (ABLIS) to identify the licenses you need.

Registration Resources

Use these official resources to help with your business registration:

  • Business Registration Service - Register your business name, company, and tax registrations in one place
  • Australian Business Register - Apply for an ABN and manage your business details
  • Australian Securities and Investments Commission - Register and manage your business name

Legal Requirements

Starting a business in Australia involves several legal obligations and requirements. Make sure you understand and comply with the following:

Key legal requirements for Australian businesses

General Legal Obligations

  • Obtain an Australian Business Number (ABN)
  • Register your business name with ASIC
  • Comply with Australian Consumer Law (ACL)
  • Maintain proper business records for at least 5 years
  • Adhere to privacy and data protection laws
  • From January 2025, intentional wage underpayment becomes a criminal offense

Tax Obligations

  • Register for Goods and Services Tax (GST) if turnover exceeds $75,000
  • Lodge Business Activity Statements (BAS) quarterly or monthly
  • Set up Pay As You Go (PAYG) withholding for employees
  • Company tax rate: 30% (standard) or 25% (for eligible base rate entities)
  • Small business income tax offset available (up to $1,000)
  • Superannuation guarantee: 11% of employee earnings

Employment Law

  • Comply with Fair Work Act and National Employment Standards
  • Adhere to relevant Modern Awards or Enterprise Agreements
  • Provide safe working conditions under WHS legislation
  • Maintain workers' compensation insurance
  • New casual conversion pathway after 12 months employment
  • Right to disconnect regulations limiting after-hours contact

Permits and Licenses

  • Use ABLIS to identify required licenses for your business
  • Obtain local council permits for premises
  • Secure industry-specific licenses and certifications
  • Environmental permits for certain activities
  • Food business licenses for hospitality operations
  • Professional registrations for regulated occupations

Intellectual Property

  • Register trademarks through IP Australia (protection for 10 years)
  • Copyright protection is automatic for original works
  • Consider patent protection for new inventions
  • Protect business designs and trade secrets
  • Be aware of digital IP risks in the online environment
  • Respect others' intellectual property rights

Structure-Specific Obligations

  • Sole Trader: Personal liability for all business debts
  • Partnership: Partnership agreement recommended, joint liability
  • Company: Director duties under Corporations Act, annual reporting
  • Trust: Trustee responsibilities, separate trust accounts
  • Different record-keeping requirements for each structure
  • Structure-specific tax obligations and reporting

Industry-Specific Requirements

Different industries have specific legal requirements. Some examples include:

  • Food and Beverage: Food safety regulations, liquor licensing, nutritional information requirements
  • Financial Services: Australian Financial Services License (AFSL), anti-money laundering obligations
  • Healthcare: Professional registration, health records management, therapeutic goods regulations
  • Construction: Building codes and standards, occupational licensing, home warranty insurance
  • E-commerce: Online consumer protection, electronic transactions regulations, international trade considerations
  • Retail: Point of sale systems for GST, trading stock requirements, consumer guarantees

Always consult with industry-specific regulatory bodies and seek professional legal advice for your particular business activities.

Compliance and Reporting

Maintaining ongoing compliance is essential for Australian businesses:

  • Conduct regular compliance reviews to ensure you meet all obligations
  • Keep accurate financial records for at least 5 years
  • Lodge tax returns and activity statements by due dates
  • Update business details with relevant authorities when changes occur
  • Maintain required insurances (public liability, professional indemnity, etc.)
  • Stay informed about regulatory changes affecting your business

Non-compliance can result in financial penalties, legal proceedings, and reputational damage.

Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about starting and running a business in Australia:

Do I need an ABN to start a business in Australia?

Yes, an Australian Business Number (ABN) is essential for almost all businesses operating in Australia. While not legally mandatory in all cases, without an ABN:

  • Other businesses must withhold 47% from payments to you
  • You cannot register a business name
  • You cannot register for GST
  • You may have difficulty dealing with other businesses

ABN registration is free through the Australian Business Register.

What's the difference between a business name and a company name?

A business name is simply a name under which you conduct your business. It doesn't create a separate legal entity. You register a business name with ASIC if you're a sole trader, partnership, or trust trading under a name other than your own.

A company name belongs to a company, which is a separate legal entity registered under the Corporations Act. When you register a company with ASIC, you receive an Australian Company Number (ACN) and the company name is registered automatically.

Key differences:

  • Business name registration doesn't provide legal ownership or exclusive rights to that name
  • Company registration creates a separate legal entity with its own rights and obligations
  • A company can own assets, sue and be sued in its own name
When do I need to register for GST?

You must register for Goods and Services Tax (GST) if:

  • Your business has a GST turnover of $75,000 or more annually ($150,000 or more for non-profit organizations)
  • You provide taxi travel or ride-sourcing services, regardless of turnover
  • You want to claim fuel tax credits for your business

You can register voluntarily if your turnover is below these thresholds. Once registered, you must:

  • Charge GST (currently 10%) on taxable sales
  • Claim credits for GST included in the price of business purchases
  • Lodge Business Activity Statements (BAS) regularly

Register for GST through the Australian Taxation Office or the Business Registration Service.

What business structure is best for a small startup?

The best structure depends on your specific circumstances, but for many small startups, a sole trader structure offers several advantages:

  • Simplest and least expensive to set up and maintain
  • Minimal regulatory requirements
  • Complete control over business decisions
  • Easy to change to a different structure as your business grows

However, consider a company structure if:

  • You want to limit your personal liability
  • You plan to seek external investment
  • You expect significant growth or high revenue
  • Your business involves higher risks

It's recommended to consult with a business advisor or accountant before deciding on a structure.

What licenses or permits might I need for my business?

The licenses and permits required depend on your business type, activities, and location. Common requirements include:

  • Local council permits for premises, signage, or outdoor seating
  • Industry-specific licenses (e.g., food handling, liquor, childcare)
  • Professional registrations for regulated occupations
  • Environmental permits for certain activities
  • Planning and development approvals

Use the Australian Business License and Information Service (ABLIS) to identify the specific licenses and permits required for your business.

What are the key tax deadlines for Australian businesses?

Important tax deadlines for Australian businesses include:

  • Business Activity Statements (BAS):
    • Quarterly: 28 days after the end of each quarter
    • Monthly: 21 days after the end of each month
  • Income Tax Returns:
    • Sole traders: October 31 (or later with a tax agent)
    • Companies: By the 15th day of the 7th month after the end of their income year
    • Trusts: October 31 (or later with a tax agent)
  • PAYG Withholding Annual Report: August 14
  • Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT) Return: May 21
  • Superannuation Guarantee Contributions: 28 days after the end of each quarter

Note: Specific dates may vary if they fall on weekends or public holidays.

How do I protect my business name and brand?

To protect your business name and brand in Australia:

  • Business Name Registration: Register with ASIC (provides no legal protection but is required)
  • Trademark Registration: Register with IP Australia for exclusive rights to use your brand name, logo, or slogan
  • Domain Name Registration: Secure relevant .com.au and .au domains
  • Social Media Handles: Claim your business name across relevant platforms
  • Copyright: Automatic protection for original creative works (no registration required)

A registered trademark provides the strongest legal protection for your brand. Visit IP Australia to search for existing trademarks and apply for registration.

Business Terms Glossary

Term Definition
ABN Australian Business Number - An 11-digit identifier issued by the Australian Business Register.
ACN Australian Company Number - A unique 9-digit number issued to companies registered under the Corporations Act.
ASIC Australian Securities and Investments Commission - The national regulator of companies and financial services.
BAS Business Activity Statement - A form submitted to the ATO to report and pay various tax obligations.
GST Goods and Services Tax - A 10% tax applied to most goods and services sold in Australia.
PAYG Pay As You Go - A system for paying tax instalments throughout the year rather than a lump sum.
TFN Tax File Number - A unique identifier issued by the ATO to individuals and entities.
FBT Fringe Benefits Tax - A tax paid by employers on certain benefits provided to employees.
Superannuation A compulsory system of retirement savings where employers contribute a percentage of an employee's earnings.
Pty Ltd Proprietary Limited - A type of private company structure in Australia with limited liability.
